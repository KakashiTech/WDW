# Fourier Bispectrum for Cₙ/Dₙ Discrimination — Theory

## 1. Mathematical Framework

### 1.1 Notation

Let $x \in \mathbb{R}^n$ be a real discrete signal with $n$ samples (1-indexed). Define:

- **DFT**: $\hat{x}_\omega = \sum_{j=0}^{n-1} x_{j+1} \cdot e^{-2\pi i \omega j / n}$, $\omega = 0, \ldots, n-1$
- **1-indexed DFT**: $\hat{x}_\omega$ for $\omega = 1, \ldots, n$ where $\omega=1$ corresponds to DC ($\exp(0)$), $\omega=2$ to frequency $k=1$, etc.
- **Spectral weights**: $A_\omega \in \mathbb{C}$, learned parameters
- **Modulated spectrum**: $\tilde{x}_\omega = A_\omega \cdot \hat{x}_\omega$

### 1.2 Group Actions

**Cyclic group $C_n$** generated by shift $T$:
$$(T^k x)[i] = x[(i-k) \bmod n] \quad \text{(1-indexed)}$$

Under DFT: $\widehat{T^k x}_\omega = \hat{x}_\omega \cdot e^{-2\pi i (\omega-1) k / n}$

**Dihedral group $D_n$** generated by shift $T$ and reflection $R$:
$$(Rx)[i] = x[n-i+2]$$

For real signals, reflection is equivalent to conjugation in Fourier domain:
$\widehat{Rx}_\omega = \hat{x}_{n-\omega+2} = \overline{\hat{x}_\omega}$

### 1.3 Time-Reversal Pairs

Our dataset consists of pairs $(x, \text{rev}(x))$ where:
$$\text{rev}(x)[i] = x[n-i+1]$$

Key observation: $\text{rev}(x) = R x$ when the reflection is about the first element:
$$(Rx)[i] = x[n-i+2] \implies (Rx)[i] = \text{rev}(x)[i-1]$$

For cyclic groups (where indexing is modulo $n$), $\text{rev}(x)$ and $R x$ differ by a shift:
$$\text{rev}(x) = T^{-1}(R x)$$

Since the bispectrum is shift-invariant (proved below), $\text{bispec}(\text{rev}(x)) = \text{bispec}(R x)$.
This is the source of the $D_n$ confusion.

## 2. Bispectrum Features

### 2.1 Definition

For frequency $\omega \in \{1, \ldots, n\}$:

$$B_z(\omega) = \tilde{x}_\omega \cdot \tilde{x}_2 \cdot \overline{\tilde{x}_{\text{mod}(\omega, n) + 1}}$$

where:
- $\tilde{x}_\omega = A_\omega \cdot \hat{x}_\omega$ (modulated spectrum)
- $\tilde{x}_2$ is the first non-DC frequency (index 2, corresponding to $k=1$)
- $\text{mod}(\omega, n) + 1$ gives cyclic wrapping: $\omega+1$ for $\omega < n$, $1$ for $\omega = n$

The feature vector is:
$$B(x) = [\operatorname{Re}(B_z(1)), \ldots, \operatorname{Re}(B_z(n)), \operatorname{Im}(B_z(1)), \ldots, \operatorname{Im}(B_z(n))] \in \mathbb{R}^{2n}$$

### 2.2 Why Index 2?

The choice of $\tilde{x}_2$ (first non-DC frequency) is not arbitrary. The phase factor under shift $T^k$ is:
- $\tilde{x}_\omega \to \tilde{x}_\omega \cdot e^{-2\pi i (\omega-1) k / n}$
- $\tilde{x}_2 \to \tilde{x}_2 \cdot e^{-2\pi i (1) k / n}$ (always picks up factor $e^{-2\pi i k / n}$)
- $\overline{\tilde{x}_{\omega+1}} \to \overline{\tilde{x}_{\omega+1}} \cdot e^{+2\pi i (\omega) k / n}$ (for $\omega < n$)

The product $e^{-2\pi i(\omega-1)k/n} \cdot e^{-2\pi i(1)k/n} \cdot e^{+2\pi i(\omega)k/n}$ cancels exactly.

If we used any other reference index $r$, the phase would be:
$e^{-2\pi i(\omega-1)k/n} \cdot e^{-2\pi i(r-1)k/n} \cdot e^{+2\pi i(\text{mod}(\omega,n))k/n}$

For $\omega < n$: $= e^{-2\pi i(\omega-1+r-1-\omega)k/n} = e^{-2\pi i(r-2)k/n} \neq 1$ for $r \neq 2$.

Only $r=2$ achieves perfect cancellation.

## 3. Shift Invariance — Proof

**Theorem**: For all $k \in \mathbb{Z}$, $B(T^k x) = B(x)$.

**Proof**:

Let $y = T^k x$. Then $\hat{y}_\omega = \hat{x}_\omega \cdot e^{-2\pi i (\omega-1) k / n}$.

The modulated spectrum is:
$$\tilde{y}_\omega = A_\omega \cdot \hat{y}_\omega = A_\omega \cdot \hat{x}_\omega \cdot e^{-2\pi i (\omega-1) k / n} = \tilde{x}_\omega \cdot e^{-2\pi i (\omega-1) k / n}$$

Then:
$$B_y(\omega) = \tilde{y}_\omega \cdot \tilde{y}_2 \cdot \overline{\tilde{y}_{\text{mod}(\omega, n) + 1}}$$

Substituting:
$$= \tilde{x}_\omega \cdot e^{-2\pi i (\omega-1) k / n} \cdot \tilde{x}_2 \cdot e^{-2\pi i (1) k / n} \cdot \overline{\tilde{x}_{\text{mod}(\omega, n) + 1}} \cdot e^{+2\pi i \cdot \text{mod}(\omega, n) \cdot k / n}$$

The phase factor is:
$$\exp\left(-2\pi i k \cdot \frac{(\omega-1) + 1 - \text{mod}(\omega, n)}{n}\right)$$

For $\omega < n$: $\text{mod}(\omega, n) = \omega$, so phase $= \exp(0) = 1$.

For $\omega = n$: $\text{mod}(n, n) = 0$, so phase $= \exp(-2\pi i k \cdot n / n) = \exp(-2\pi i k) = 1$.

Therefore $B_y(\omega) = B_x(\omega)$ for all $\omega$. $\square$

**Empirical verification**: $\|B(\text{shifted}) - B(\text{orig})\| \approx 2.8 \times 10^{-15}$ (float64 precision).

## 4. $D_n$ Sensitivity — Proof

**Theorem**: If $A$ is $D_n$-symmetric ($A_\omega = \overline{A_{n-\omega+2}}$ for all $\omega$), then
$B(Rx) = \overline{B(x)}$ where conjugation is element-wise on $B_z(\omega)$.
For asymmetric $A$, the change is non-trivial.

**Proof**:

Under reflection $R$, $\widehat{Rx}_\omega = \hat{x}_{n-\omega+2} = \overline{\hat{x}_\omega}$ (for real signals).

The modulated spectrum becomes:
$$\widetilde{Rx}_\omega = A_\omega \cdot \overline{\hat{x}_\omega} = \overline{\overline{A_\omega} \cdot \hat{x}_\omega}$$

For the bispectrum:
$$B_{Rx}(\omega) = \widetilde{Rx}_\omega \cdot \widetilde{Rx}_2 \cdot \overline{\widetilde{Rx}_{\text{mod}(\omega, n) + 1}}$$
$$= A_\omega \overline{\hat{x}_\omega} \cdot A_2 \overline{\hat{x}_2} \cdot \overline{A_{\text{mod}(\omega, n) + 1} \overline{\hat{x}_{\text{mod}(\omega, n) + 1}}}$$
$$= A_\omega A_2 \overline{A_{\text{mod}(\omega, n) + 1}} \cdot \overline{\hat{x}_\omega \hat{x}_2 \overline{\hat{x}_{\text{mod}(\omega, n) + 1}}}$$
$$= \frac{A_\omega A_2 \overline{A_{\text{mod}(\omega, n) + 1}}}{\overline{A_\omega A_2 \overline{A_{\text{mod}(\omega, n) + 1}}}} \cdot \overline{\tilde{x}_\omega \tilde{x}_2 \overline{\tilde{x}_{\text{mod}(\omega, n) + 1}}}$$

When $A$ is $D_n$-symmetric ($A_\omega = \overline{A_{n-\omega+2}}$), the ratio is real and positive for all $\omega$, giving $B_{Rx}(\omega) = \overline{B_x(\omega)}$.

For asymmetric $A$, the features change non-trivially — the ratio is a complex phase that varies per frequency.

**Empirical verification**: $\|B(Rx) - B(x)\| \approx 5.6$ for random $A$ (factor $\sim 5\times$ change).

## 5. $C_n \neq D_n$ Accuracy Gap

### 5.1 Mechanism for Time-Reversal Pairs

For time-reversal pairs $(x, \text{rev}(x))$:
1. $\text{rev}(x) = T^{-1}(R x)$ (reflection followed by shift)
2. Since bispectrum is shift-invariant: $B(\text{rev}(x)) = B(R x)$
3. Under $D_n$ evaluation, $R x$ is classified as $\text{rev}(x)$
4. The classifier outputs the time-reversed class label
5. Hence $D_n$ accuracy = 0% (all inputs misclassified as their time-reversed pair)

### 5.2 Why 100pp Gap is Robust

The gap does NOT depend on $A$ asymmetry:
- At $A = I$ (identity): gap = 100pp
- At $\lambda_{\text{asym}} = -10$ (max asymmetry 0.374): gap = 100pp

The gap is INHERENT to the bispectrum $\times$ time-reversal dataset structure:
- The bispectrum provides shift invariance (necessary for generalization)
- The time-reversal structure couples reflection with class membership
- The combination makes $D_n$ accuracy identically zero

### 5.3 Why Power Spectrum Fails

The power spectrum $|\hat{x}_\omega|^2$ is both $C_n$-invariant and $D_n$-invariant:
$$|\widehat{T^k x}_\omega|^2 = |\hat{x}_\omega|^2 \quad \text{and} \quad |\widehat{R x}_\omega|^2 = |\hat{x}_{n-\omega+2}|^2 = |\hat{x}_\omega|^2$$

Since time-reversed signals have identical power spectra:
$$|\widehat{\text{rev}(x)}_\omega|^2 = |\hat{x}_\omega|^2$$

The power spectrum alone CANNOT distinguish between a signal and its time reversal.
Within-pair accuracy = 50% (random).

## 6. Exact Recovery

Recovery is algebraically exact:
$$\tilde{x}_\omega = A_\omega \cdot \hat{x}_\omega \implies \hat{x}_\omega = \frac{\tilde{x}_\omega}{A_\omega}$$
$$x = \text{IFFT}\left(\frac{\tilde{x}_\omega}{A_\omega}\right)$$

For any non-zero $A_\omega$, this is an identity. MSE $\approx 10^{-33}$ is float64 precision.
